Sometimes it feels important to write something, even when there is nothing in particular to say. Words pile up, sentences drift slightly off course, and before you know it you have a paragraph that looks busy but does very little. That can be oddly satisfying in its own way. You might start talking about the weather, then move on to how time seems to pass faster on weekdays than weekends, and somehow end up thinking about whether toast tastes better when it is cut diagonally.

There is also a tendency to add details that sound meaningful but are mostly filler. For example, mentioning a cup of coffee that has gone cold while sitting on a desk next to some half finished notes. Or describing a vague plan to be productive later, even though everyone knows that later is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence. None of this really matters, but it fills space nicely and gives the impression of motion.

By the end, the text feels complete enough to exist, even if it never actually said anything. That is probably fine. Not everything needs a point.
